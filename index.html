Global Analysis of a Dynamical Model for Tuberculosis Transmission
with a General Contact Function

A Lyapunov-based approach to TB epidemic thresholds

Samuel Bowong, Jean Jules Tewa

Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research, Germany

Motivation & Contact Function

  • TB burden: One-third of world's population infected with M. tuberculosis → 2–3 million deaths/year.
  • Accelerators: HIV, MDR-TB, poverty, crowding, limited healthcare.
  • Key idea: Contact rate depends on population size $N$ → general function $\beta(N)$.

📐 Contact function forms

Mass action
$$\beta(N) = \beta$$
Incidence: $\beta S I$ (small populations)
Standard incidence
$$\beta(N) = \frac{\beta}{N}$$
Incidence: $\beta S I / N$ (large populations)
Saturating (Heesterbeek-Metz)
$$\beta(N) = \frac{bN}{1 + bN + \sqrt{1 + 2bN}}$$
Linear for small $N$, saturates for large $N$
✅ Assumptions: $\beta(N) > 0$, $\beta'(N) \le 0$, $(N\beta(N))' \ge 0$
(per-capita contacts non-increasing, total contacts non-decreasing)

SEI Model Structure

$$\begin{cases} \dot{S} = \Lambda - \beta(N)SI - \mu S, \\ \dot{E} = \beta(N)(1-p)SI + r_2I - [\mu + k(1-r_1)]E, \\ \dot{I} = \beta(N)pSI + k(1-r_1)E - (\mu + d + \delta + r_2)I \end{cases}$$
🔵 Compartments
$S$ – Susceptible  |  $E$ – Exposed (latent)
$I$ – Infectious (active TB)
⚙️ Parameters
$\Lambda$: recruitment   $\mu$: natural death
$p$: fast progression   $k$: reactivation
$r_1$: chemoprophylaxis   $r_2$: treatment
$d$: disease-induced death   $\delta$: emigration
Interpretation: New infections occur at rate $\beta(N) S I$. Fraction $p$ goes directly to $I$ (fast), rest to $E$. Latent individuals may reactivate ($k$), and treated infectious return to $E$.

Basic Reproduction Number $\mathcal{R}_0$

$$\mathcal{R}_0 = \frac{\beta(S_0)S_0[\mu p + k(1 - r_1)]}{(\mu + d + \delta)[\mu + k(1 - r_1)] + \mu r_2}, \quad S_0 = \frac{\Lambda}{\mu}$$
  • $\mathcal{R}_0$ = average number of secondary infections from one infectious case in a fully susceptible population.
  • $\mathcal{R}_0 < 1$ → Disease-Free Equilibrium (DFE) $P_0=(S_0,0,0)$ is locally asymptotically stable (LAS).
  • $\mathcal{R}_0 > 1$ → DFE unstable, a unique endemic equilibrium emerges.
📌 Threshold property: The fate of the epidemic is fully determined by $\mathcal{R}_0$.

Global Stability of DFE

Theorem: If $\mathcal{R}_0 \le 1$, the disease-free equilibrium $P_0 = (S_0,0,0)$ is globally asymptotically stable (GAS).
Lyapunov function:
$$V(E,I) = k(1 - r_1)E + [\mu + k(1 - r_1)]I$$
Time derivative:
$$\dot{V} \le \bigl[r_2\mu + (\mu + k(1 - r_1))(\mu + d + \delta)\bigr](\mathcal{R}_0 - 1)I \le 0$$
By LaSalle's invariance principle, all trajectories in the feasible region converge to $P_0$.
Meaning: No matter the initial outbreak size (within biologically relevant domain), TB eventually disappears when $\mathcal{R}_0 \le 1$.

Endemic Equilibrium

Existence & uniqueness: When $\mathcal{R}_0 > 1$, there exists a unique positive endemic equilibrium $P^* = (S^*, E^*, I^*)$.
Global stability under conditions:
$$\frac{S}{S^*} \le \frac{E}{E^*}, \quad \frac{S}{S^*} \le \frac{I}{I^*}$$
Lyapunov function (Volterra type):
$$U = S - S^*\ln S + A(E - E^*\ln E) + B(I - I^*\ln I)$$
with $A,B > 0$ chosen appropriately. $\dot{U} \le 0$ and equality only at $P^*$ → $P^*$ is GAS in the interior.
💡 Numerics suggest the conditions are sufficient but may be relaxed; simulations always show convergence to $P^*$.

Numerical Results & Conclusion

📊 Three contact scenarios tested
✅ Mass action $\beta(N)=\beta$
✅ Standard incidence $\beta(N)=\beta/N$
✅ Saturating function (Heesterbeek-Metz)
🎯 Threshold behavior confirmed
$\mathcal{R}_0 < 1$ → convergence to DFE (disease eliminated)
$\mathcal{R}_0 > 1$ → convergence to endemic equilibrium (persistence)
🔬 Conclusion:
  • Global dynamics completely determined by $\mathcal{R}_0$.
  • Global asymptotic stability proven via Lyapunov functions + LaSalle.
  • Numerical simulations (Cameroon data) perfectly match theoretical predictions.
  • The framework with general contact function $\beta(N)$ unifies mass action, standard, and saturating incidence.
